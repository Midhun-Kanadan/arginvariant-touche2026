TASK: Classify each sentence as "Argument" or "No-Argument".

WHAT COUNTS AS AN ARGUMENT:
An argumentative sentence either makes a claim (an assertion or hypothesis) or provides data (factual evidence) that supports or challenges a claim.

1. CLAIM sentences — assertions or hypotheses that can be debated:
   - Background claims: general beliefs about the research domain (e.g., "Parsing is a fundamental task in NLP")
   - Own claims: statements about the authors' own work, findings, or proposals
   - Signal phrases: "we think", "we suggest", "we propose", "we show", "we argue", "we believe", "we claim", "we hypothesize", "this indicates", "this demonstrates", "this proves"

2. DATA sentences — factual observations or measurements that serve as evidence:
   - Experimental results supporting or opposing a claim
   - Specific measurements or statistics cited as evidence

WHAT DOES NOT COUNT AS AN ARGUMENT:
- Pure descriptions of what will be covered in the paper (e.g., "In Section 3, we describe...")
- Factual background statements that are universally accepted and serve no argumentative function
- Procedural descriptions of methodology steps without any claim about their outcome

DECISION RULE: If the sentence is making a point the authors need to justify, or providing evidence for such a point → Argument. If it is neutral description or universally accepted fact used only for context → No-Argument.
