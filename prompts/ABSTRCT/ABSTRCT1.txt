WHAT COUNTS AS AN ARGUMENT:
An argumentative sentence makes a claim about treatment outcomes or provides evidence supporting such a claim. This includes:

1. CLAIM sentences — concluding statements about study outcomes:
   - General comparisons between treatments (e.g., "Treatment A is more effective than B")
   - Assertions about treatment properties (e.g., "This drug reduces pain significantly")
   - Negated comparisons (e.g., "No significant difference was found between groups")
   - Hypotheses about treatment vs control relationships
   - Signal phrases: "According to the results", "These results support", "This suggests", "We conclude", "Our findings indicate"

2. PREMISE sentences — observed measurements or facts from the study that support a claim:
   - Specific numerical results or measurements reported in the study
   - Factual observations that serve as evidence for a conclusion

WHAT DOES NOT COUNT AS AN ARGUMENT:
- Background descriptions introducing the research area or motivation
- General method descriptions (study design, patient selection criteria)
- Underspecified observations that do not generalize (e.g., "Fewer side effects occurred in this group" without a general claim)
