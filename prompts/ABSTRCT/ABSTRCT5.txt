ANNOTATION GUIDELINE:
A sentence is an ARGUMENT if it CONCLUDES or EVALUATES. A sentence is NOT AN ARGUMENT if it REPORTS or DESCRIBES.

(A) CLAIM — a sentence that evaluates or concludes:
  - Asserts a treatment property using a stative construction: "was well tolerated", "is effective", "is not inferior to", "is feasible", "was favorable"
  - Concludes that a treatment has NO meaningful effect: "had no effect on X", "did not produce a statistically significant advantage", "does not prolong survival", "resulted in no improvement", "no correlation was found", "could not be identified"
  - Asserts a general disease property or established association: "Fatigue has a significant impact on QOL", "X is associated with poor prognosis"
  - Asserts the state of the field: "the role of X remains controversial", "evidence is conflicting", "remains unclear", "has emerged as the standard"
  - Makes a recommendation or calls for further work: "Further studies are needed", "should be considered", "is necessary to confirm", "warrant another trial"
  - Signals a conclusion: "These results suggest...", "According to these results...", "This confirms that..."

(B) EVIDENCE — a specific finding from the current study presented as a concise key result:
  - Compares two study arms with brief quantified values: "Group A had X vs. Group B had Y"
  - The primary purpose of the sentence is to establish the main result, not to enumerate detailed statistics

THE KEY DISTINCTION — Reporting vs. Concluding:

NOT-ARGUMENT (Reporting): A sentence that describes what was OBSERVED or MEASURED using a dynamic action verb, even if "significantly" is present. Pattern:
  "[Subject] [significantly] [improved / reduced / decreased / increased / achieved / demonstrated / experienced / showed / was higher / was lower / was more frequent] [measured outcome] [in Group A] [compared to Group B / versus Group B] [(P=...) (HR=...) (CI=...)]"
  The word "significantly" here means "statistically significant" — it describes the data, it does not make an evaluative conclusion.
  → "Letrozole significantly improved distant DFS (HR=0.60; P=0.002)" → NOT-ARGUMENT
  → "Fat mass had significantly decreased by month 12 in the exemestane group (P<0.01)" → NOT-ARGUMENT
  → "PFS was significantly longer for fulvestrant 500 mg than 250 mg (HR=0.80; P=.006)" → NOT-ARGUMENT

ARGUMENT (Concluding): A sentence that states a GENERAL EVALUATION or a NULL CONCLUSION without enumerating the raw statistics:
  → "Letrozole was more effective than placebo for DFS" → ARGUMENT (evaluative conclusion)
  → "Treatment X had no effect on the primary outcome" → ARGUMENT (null conclusion = claim that it failed)
  → "No correlation between lung expansion and clinical outcome was found" → ARGUMENT (null conclusion)
  → "The treatment was well tolerated" → ARGUMENT (evaluative)

ADDITIONAL NOT-ARGUMENT cases:
  - States the study objective: "To compare X with Y", "To assess X", "To determine X"
  - Describes study design or methodology (randomization, blinding, treatment schedule)
  - Reports patient enrollment or baseline demographics
  - Provides a definition: "Success was defined as..."
  - Cites the findings of a specific named previous trial: "In the [named] trial, X was shown to improve Y"
  - Null results stated with raw P-values only: "No significant difference was observed (P=.49)", "P=0.5"
