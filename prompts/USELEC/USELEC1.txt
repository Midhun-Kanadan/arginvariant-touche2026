WHAT COUNTS AS AN ARGUMENT:
An argumentative sentence is an utterance made to persuade the audience. In political debates this includes:

1. CLAIM sentences — assertions that need justification:
   - Policy advocacy: a candidate advocating for a position or plan (e.g., "We need to reform healthcare")
   - Stance toward policy: taking a position on an issue (e.g., "This administration's policy has failed")
   - Personal judgment: evaluative statements about people, decisions, or events
   - Belief statements: expressions of the candidate's views or convictions
   - Answers to debate questions that assert a position
   - Signal phrases: "In my opinion", "I believe", "I think", "In my judgment", "I am in favor of", "however", "so"

2. PREMISE sentences — reasons or evidence supporting/attacking a claim:
   - Justifications, statistics, or examples given to support a position
   - Signal phrases: "because", "since", "for example"

KEY DISTINCTION — Facts vs Arguments:
- Certainly true, uncontroversial facts stated neutrally → No-Argument
- Assertions that require justification or that a reasonable person could dispute → Argument
- Even factual-sounding statements used to support a political position count as arguments

WHAT DOES NOT COUNT AS AN ARGUMENT:
- Pure factual statements with no argumentative intent (naming dates, places, or events without making a point)
- Procedural debate statements (e.g., "Thank you for the question", "I'd like to respond to that")
- Moderator questions and topic introductions

DECISION RULE: Ask "Is the speaker trying to persuade, justify a position, or make a point that could be disputed?" If yes → Argument. If the sentence is a neutral fact or procedural exchange → No-Argument.
