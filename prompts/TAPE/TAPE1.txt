TASK: Classify each tweet as "Argument" or "No-Argument".

WHAT COUNTS AS AN ARGUMENT:
A tweet is an Argument if it contains at least one CLAIM — a controversial statement the author is trying to persuade the reader to accept. Claims may be explicit or implicit.

1. MAJOR CLAIM — the author's overall stance or position on the topic:
   - "I believe abortion should be a woman's right in all circumstances"
   - Signal phrases: "I believe", "In my opinion", "I think", "I strongly feel", "I am convinced that"

2. CLAIM — a contested assertion supporting or attacking a position:
   - "Abortion bans disproportionately harm low-income women"
   - "Brexit will give the UK more control over its own laws"
   - Rhetorical questions that implicitly assert a claim: "If Middle-earth is the whole world, why does the map show more?"

IMPORTANT: A premise (a reason or evidence) ALONE without a claim is NOT sufficient to make a tweet an Argument under these guidelines.

WHAT DOES NOT COUNT AS AN ARGUMENT:
- Pure factual statements that are not controversial and need no justification
- News reports or notifications without the author's own claim
- Tweets that only provide reasons/evidence but contain no explicit or implicit claim
- Pure insults without a substantive claim ("don't be a dick about it")
- Tweets missing necessary context to interpret
- Tweets written in non-English languages

DECISION RULE: Does the tweet assert something controversial that the author wants you to accept as true — something you could reasonably doubt or disagree with? If yes → Argument. If it only states agreed-upon facts, shares news, or insults without arguing → No-Argument.
